Why These Two Sources,
Together

The Stanford curriculum tells us how to build sustainability leaders. The Cambridge book tells us what is clinically happening inside young people when they confront climate reality. Neither is sufficient alone. Together, they create something unprecedented: a curriculum that develops the inner capacity required to become a genuine sustainability leader ‚Äî not just the competencies.

Novy, Banerjee & Matson ¬∑ MDPI Sustainability 2021

The "New Leader" framework from Stanford

The Stanford curriculum identifies four concentric rings of the sustainability leader: Identity (values, purpose), Perspective (systems lens), Capability (skills, process sense), and Agency (sense of possibility, collaborative action). The curriculum explicitly argues that developing competencies alone is insufficient ‚Äî you must transform the whole person. This becomes our scaffolding.

Haase & Hudson (eds.) ¬∑ Cambridge University Press 2024

Clinical foundations of youth climate distress

The Cambridge book reveals that youth climate distress is multi-layered and developmentally specific ‚Äî touching psychiatric, neurobiological, psychoanalytic, trauma, and CBT dimensions. Young people face unique developmental stakes: climate threatens their identity formation, future imagination, and sense of generational justice. Ignoring this clinical reality produces leaders who burn out, freeze, or rage without traction.

The synthesis

Emotional competency IS sustainability competency

The key insight: the Stanford "New Leader" model requires resilience, adaptive capacity, tolerance of uncertainty, and collaborative trust. These are not soft extras ‚Äî they are precisely what the Cambridge clinical literature shows is destroyed by unprocessed climate distress. You cannot build a systems thinker whose system is perpetually dysregulated. Processing the distress is leadership development.

Design principle

Meaning-focused coping drives both well-being and action

Chapter 14 of the Cambridge book (Ojala & Chen) makes the empirical case that meaning-focused coping ‚Äî as distinct from problem-focused or emotion-focused ‚Äî produces both better mental health outcomes and greater climate engagement in young people. This becomes the red thread connecting every module: we are not teaching emotional management as a precondition to leadership. We are teaching that meaning-making is the leadership act.

Your Leverage Point Map

Sunnie guides users through a simplified causal loop ‚Äî starting with one climate issue they care about, mapping 3 causes and 3 effects, then identifying one leverage point where they or others could intervene. ACT Defusion ¬∑ Behavioral

News as Data, Not Verdict

A structured experiment: users select one climate news story and practice the ACT skill of defusion ‚Äî treating the story as information rather than as a definitive statement about the future. Sunnie prompts: "What does this story tell us is happening? What does it NOT tell us about what's possible?" Rebuilds epistemic confidence in one's own interpretive capacity.

Cambridge Ch.5 (Neuropsychiatric Perspectives): the anxiety system is pattern-matching and future-projecting ‚Äî climate news activates threat-detection pathways disproportionately. ACT defusion interrupts the fusion between information and catastrophic prediction, restoring the user's sense of interpretive agency.

Module V ¬∑ Cambridge Ch.14: Meaning-Focused Coping

From Paralysis
to Purpose

Chapter 14 (Ojala & Chen) is the empirical heart of this module ‚Äî and one of the most strategically important contributions of the Cambridge book. It distinguishes three coping orientations: problem-focused (what can I fix?), emotion-focused (how do I feel less bad?), and meaning-focused (what does this mean and what does it call me toward?). Meaning-focused coping is the only orientation empirically shown to produce both better mental well-being AND higher climate engagement in young people simultaneously. This is the bridge between healing and leadership.

Sunnie introduces constructive hope (Ojala's framework) ‚Äî as distinct from wishful thinking or toxic positivity. Evidence Base

Every module activity is traceable to a specific chapter, study, or framework. The two primary sources are bolded; supporting evidence follows.

Primary Source ¬∑ Stanford University ¬∑ MDPI Sustainability 2021

Novy, J.W., Banerjee, B. & Matson, P. ‚Äî "A Core Curriculum for Sustainability Leadership"

The foundational curriculum design paper for Stanford's Change Leadership for Sustainability Program. Developed through 100+ empathy interviews with global leaders, faculty working sessions, and a faculty-CEO Roundtable. Introduces the "New Leader" four-ring model (Identity ‚Üí Perspective ‚Üí Capability ‚Üí Agency) as the scaffolding for our six modules. The paper's explicit normative goal of "intergenerational well-being" becomes the capstone of Module VI. The three curriculum elements ‚Äî systems understanding, decision-making for change, and systemic innovation ‚Äî map directly to Modules II, V, and VI respectively.

Used in: All modules (scaffolding). Primary: Modules I, II, V, VI.
Primary Source ¬∑ Cambridge University Press 2024

Haase, E. & Hudson, K. (eds.) ‚Äî "Climate Change and Youth Mental Health: Multidisciplinary Perspectives"

The most comprehensive clinical text on youth climate distress available. Part I provides conceptual foundations across nine chapters covering psychiatric, developmental, neuropsychiatric, psychoanalytic, trauma/dissociation, and CBT perspectives. Part II covers therapist practice, ecological/intersectional approaches, pediatric, legal, coping science, social-ecological systems, and intergenerational dimensions. Appendix B (Costs and Benefits of Activism Scale) is integrated directly into Module V. Each chapter maps to a specific module and set of Flourish activities.

Used in: All modules. Primary chapters: Ch.3‚Äì8 (Module III), Ch.10‚Äì11 (Module IV), Ch.14 (Module V), Ch.15 (Module VI).
Cambridge Ch.14 ¬∑ Ojala & Chen

Meaning-Focused Coping and Constructive Hope in Young People

Provides the empirical foundation for Module V. Establishes that meaning-focused coping ‚Äî as distinct from problem-focused or emotion-focused ‚Äî is the only coping orientation associated with both improved mental well-being AND increased climate engagement in young people simultaneously. Introduces constructive hope as an evidence-based alternative to both toxic positivity and despair. Reviews studies showing collective climate engagement generates hope, not the reverse.

Primary for: Module V.
Cambridge Ch.15 ¬∑ Crandon, Thomas & Scott

Social-Ecological Perspectives on Youth Climate Distress

Uses Bronfenbrenner's bioecological model to map factors that exacerbate or protect against youth climate distress across micro (family, peers), meso (school, community), techno (technology, media), exo (government), and macro (culture) systems. Identifies peer relationships as the most consistently protective meso-level factor. Establishes that resilience requires systemic engagement, not only individual skill-building.

Primary for: Module VI.
Cambridge Ch.10 ¬∑ Therapists' Perspectives

ACT and Psychodynamic Approaches to Climate-Distressed Youth

Describes clinical practice from ACT and psychodynamic frameworks specifically applied to climate-distressed young people. Identifies secure attachment, conscious relationship to nature, tolerance for uncertainty, psychoeducation, resilience skills, and creative/play-based expression as the core intervention toolkit. Notes that clinicians must also process their own climate emotions ‚Äî a parallel process awareness that Sunnie's design should reflect.

Primary for: Module IV. Supporting: Modules I, III.
Supporting ¬∑ Baudon & Jachens ¬∑ PMC 2021

A Scoping Review of Interventions for Eco-Anxiety

Reviews 34 records and identifies five major intervention categories: resilience-building, action-encouraging, social connection, nature connection, and grief-informed approaches. Confirms creative expression, grief rituals, and group-based peer support as evidence-supported modalities. Used across multiple modules as cross-validating evidence.

Supporting: Modules I, III, IV, VI.
Supporting ¬∑ Lindqvist et al. ¬∑ ScienceDirect 2023

Climate-Specific ICBT ‚Äî First Randomized Controlled Trial

The only published RCT on internet-delivered CBT specifically designed for climate distress. Effect sizes: depression d=0.87, stress d=0.76, quality of life d=0.79 over 8 weeks. Notably, the ICBT group also showed increased pro-environmental behavior ‚Äî supporting the Cambridge Ch.14 finding that treating distress unlocks action. Validates Flourish's ICBT delivery model for this population.

Supporting: All modules (delivery model validation).
Supporting ¬∑ Whitmarsh et al. ¬∑ Journal of Environmental Psychology 2022

Climate Anxiety: Predictors and Relationship to Climate Action

Large-scale study examining what predicts climate anxiety and how it relates to climate action. Finds that climate anxiety, when channeled through meaningful engagement, predicts higher pro-environmental behavior. Supports the Module V design principle that processing anxiety is not separate from building capacity for action ‚Äî it is the prerequisite.

Supporting: Module V.
